# Build articles as assets, not one-off posts

Most teams publish SEO content like campaigns. They brainstorm a topic, write a post, ship it, and move on. That creates output, but it does not create a **traffic system**.

The better model is to treat every article as a reusable asset with a clear role inside a larger demand engine:

- capture a specific search intent
- answer the query with enough depth to deserve rankings
- connect to adjacent topics through internal links
- move the visitor into a commercial next step

When your article format is driven by Markdown and frontmatter, the publishing workflow becomes fast enough to scale and structured enough to keep quality under control.

## What frontmatter should control

Your frontmatter should store the fields the page template needs for both search and editorial operations:

- `title` for the visible headline and search title
- `description` for the meta description
- `excerpt` for article cards and internal recommendations
- `publishedAt` and `updatedAt` for freshness signals
- `category` and `tags` for clustering
- `ctaLabel` and `ctaHref` for traffic routing

This gives you a clean split: frontmatter handles the metadata layer, while Markdown handles the actual thinking.

## A practical content loop

Here is the operating loop that tends to work well for service businesses:

1. pick a keyword with clear commercial adjacency
2. define the search intent before you define the headline
3. write the article to solve the query in full
4. link to one conversion page and two related supporting articles
5. refresh the article when search behavior or your offer changes

This makes content less dependent on inspiration and more dependent on process.

## What makes an SEO article template useful

The template matters because it removes variance in the parts that should be consistent:

- metadata
- page structure
- schema output
- call to action placement
- related article surfacing

It should not flatten the writing. The template standardizes the delivery, not the insight.

> If you cannot explain why an article exists in the funnel, it is probably content debt.

## Recommended page anatomy

An article page designed for traffic should usually include:

- a clear headline and subhead
- author and date metadata
- table of contents for scanability
- strong section hierarchy with `h2` and `h3`
- contextual CTA near the top and again near the end
- related content links to keep users moving

That structure helps both readers and crawlers understand the page quickly.

## Example internal linking logic

Suppose this article targets "SEO content strategy for service businesses." It should probably link outward to articles like:

- "how to build a topic cluster from customer questions"
- "service page SEO vs blog SEO"
- "when to update declining articles"

And it should link inward to one commercial destination where the visitor can take the next step.

## Why Markdown is a good authoring format

Markdown is useful here because it stays portable. You can:

- version article changes in Git
- review copy in pull requests
- keep content close to the code that renders it
- move to a headless CMS later without rewriting the page design

That is a strong setup when you want speed now without locking yourself into a brittle content stack.

## The traffic logic behind this example

This example page is not just a visual demo. It encodes a simple traffic model:

1. attract intent with a search-matched page
2. qualify the reader with a point of view
3. route them deeper through internal links
4. convert the right readers with a focused CTA

If you want content to compound, build more pages like products and fewer pages like essays.
